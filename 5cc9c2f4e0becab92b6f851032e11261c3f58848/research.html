Research
Our team onboarded into the financial sphere using generative research. We studied the traders' day and their work environment, including interactions with software. From this work, we understood traders' needs and desires from Liquidnet's software.
Overview
In this phase, we collected information about our users and "dark-pool institutional trading", which Liquidnet specializes in. Our work focused on understanding the trader (our user) and their technology (the Liquidnet 5 product).

We needed to quickly onboard ourselves in the financial world to study traders and Liquidnet's product.

Challenge

Our team had to understand traders well enough to design for them according to our project brief.

Users
Traders are core to Liquidnet's success - they execute trades, they analyze research, and they develop algorithms, all benefitting Liquidnet. Understanding trader’s goals and needs tells us where Liquidnet should target traders. We'll walk through the research we did to understand the different stakeholders in dark pool trading.
Jeevan the Equity Trader
GOALS
As an equity trader Jeevan's goals are to improve his performance in his trades, and to create innovative trading strategies.
NEEDS
Jeevan needs to be confident in his trades even if they fail, and needs to grasp high-quality data and algorithms fit his trading strategy.
Jed the Research Analyst
GOALS
Jed wants to provide useful insights on 'good' trading decisions, thus becoming a trusted resource for traders.
NEEDS
Jed needs to be aware of market direction, and must establish a rapport with traders so that his research is taken seriously.
Jane the Portfolio Manager
GOALS
Jane wants to achieve 'good' portfolio performance with low risk overall, as well as avoid information leakage at all costs.
NEEDS
Jane needs to be confident in her opinions, even if her team disagrees. She must also understand clients’ need to build portfolios.

RESEARCH METHODS
Only a few niche traders use Liquidnet or take part in dark-pool trading. As such, our team used generative research to understand the elusive dark-pool traders and proxy users that experience trading-esque work. Through these activities, we learned how traders think and act on the job.
Observation 1
Dark pools stand out
  • Our team played poker and a stock management game
  • Both games emulate the stress and timing of real trading
  • Poker is a commonly used game to train young traders
Observation 2
A trader's day and peers
  • Preliminary research gave us an idea of a hypothetical trader
  • Interviewees discussed unique trader types and teams
  • We learned that trading teams don't have standard sizes or roles
Observation 1
Trades are an 'action stream'
  • Institutional trades 'stream in' and change throughout the day
  • Change in news, pricing, and market may influence the stream
  • Traders are prepared to change actions mid-stream
Observation 2
The three phases of a trader's day
  • Open: Traders input their day's trades, hectic time
  • Mid: Most calm, ideal for smaller mid-stream actions
  • Close: Pressure rises to finish trades and report work
Observation 1
Poker is analogous to trading
  • Poker is often training for incoming traders
  • The pace and mental load of poker mimics trading
  • In-game planning mimics the portfolio management
Observation 2
Poker players struggle like traders
  • Players need to recover and gain emotional balance
  • Most time is spent disengaged from the game itself
  • More engagement can build positive feelings on wins
Observation 1
Gaming can emulate trading
  • Our team played poker and a stock management game
  • Both games emulate the stress and timing of real trading
  • We played competitively to mimic trader competition
Observation 2
Some lessons learned
  • In poker, strategy management is mentally challenging
  • Both games require strategy to succeed long-term
  • Even strategic choices can 'go bad' due to luck

After gathering a considerable amount of generative data on traders, our team synthesized findings by using the following techniques. These maps and graphs helped us collect insights and communicate our research findings with ease to our clients.
Insight 1
Collecting insights

  • External services and brokers provide 'unique' information
  • This information allows traders to improve their strategy
Insight 2
Liquidnet serves overlapping roles
  • Liquidnet serves as a broker and external analytics platform
  • Relationship managers sustain loyalty and trust with the trader
  • Proprietary data brings traders back to the Liquidnet product
Insight 1
Actions repeat
  • Pre-trade is determined by the client and/or portfolio
  • Trades are untouched unless an anomaly occurs
  • Mid-day anomalies are handled as a team
Insight 2
Emotions dip mid-day
  • The highlights of the day are the open and close
  • Mid-day is mundane, and is mostly updating prior trades
  • Open and close are both exciting and stressful
Insight 1
Screen real estate

  • Typically monitors are dedicated to a type of application
  • Liquidnet front-end is actually infrequently used
Insight 2
Front-end distractions
  • Liquidnet usually appears on-screen as pop-ups
  • These pop-ups are for immediate action or alerts
  • The front-end is often seen as a distraction to traders
Insight 1
What does Liquidnet do for traders?
  • Shows unique data through Discovery analytics
  • Builds trust with traders via relationship managers
  • Provides the most liquidity compared to the competiton
Insight 2
A trader needs...
  • To trust the information provided from software and peers
  • Unique or proprietary data to inform trading strategy
  • A robust risk management strategy for any situation
Product
With a better understanding of traders' workflow, our team studied the Liquidnet software. We learned about the target customers and the features marketed towards them. Then, we compared the target to the actual users of Liquidnet. Below are some of the generalized findings about the product.
Customers
An entire company or a single trader may purchase Liquidnet for their trading teams. When interacting wtih the customer, Liquidnet must keep the customer’s portfolio in mind.
Features
Liquidnet has acquired analytics technology to build upon its order management and execution software. They want their customers to do more than execute trades on Liquidnet.
Users
Traders often avoid Liquidnet's front-end product and features. They want Liquidnet's features to be easier to find and use, and will avoid features with ambiguous value to them.
How did we study the product?
RESEARCH METHODS
Parallel to our generative research on users, our team gathered preliminary research about Liquidnet and their competitor's offerings. This involved a competitive analysis, as well as studying Liquidnet's sequence of operations and adherence to design heuristics.
Liquidnet's advantage
  • There is a historical preference for Liquidnet, the first dark-pool
  • BIDS and ITG are cheaper software, but give less data to traders
  • Liquidnet has the best liquidity as the first dark-pool
Liquidnet's disadvantage
  • Competitors ask for a lower commission (cheaper trades)
  • Liquidnet's software is significantly more expensive
  • Competitors excel at algorithms and quick matches
Mapping workflows
  • We mapped each sequence of actions in workflow
  • Many workflows interact or connect throughout a day
  • E.g. passive match flow contains the active match flow
Patterns in workflows
  • Many workflows use the same features in slightly different ways
  • Mapping showed which workflows were most common and important
  • Active and passive trade workflows are most routine for all traders
Industry standards
  • Fintech software often emulates spreadsheets
  • Common colors are bright red, green and yellow
  • Industry standards haven't changed in the last decade
Design standards
  • Many visual heuristics were not met by Liquidnet 5
  • Color and iconography will make their software more intuitive
  • Visual hierarchy must guide trader through workflow
SYNTHESIS
Through the methods just shown and from our onsite interviews, our team found the following insights. Overall, many traders have cognitive and emotional overload from their job and their software. Traders avoid new features and experience difficult UI interactions on a daily basis.
Workflow overload
  • Traders typically master a workflow and stick to it
  • Connected workflows are not highlighted visually in Liquidnet
  • Thus, traders are reluctant to learn new workflows and features
Finding features
  • Due to of visual clutter, new features are hard to find
  • No in-app help means traders must call sales for help
  • Hidden features in Liquidnet's blotter are unused and unseen
Juggling workflows
  • Traders have to juggle many workflows and data
  • Traders have to shift workflows based on market
  • Allowing the trader to change action should be easy
Finding liquidity
  • Traders inherently will use competing applications
  • Liquidity offerings can change trader's app usage
  • Liquidnet needs to convince traders to stay with them
Information overload
  • Poor visual design and hierarchy can confuse traders
  • If traders can't execute trades, they'll switch to a competitor
  • Showing liquidity opportunities with data keeps trades
Scarcity of space
  • Screen real estate is extremely valuable and limited
  • What isn't seen isn't used or known to traders
  • When and what info is shown can convince trader to act
Users + Product
After user and product research, we understood where Liquidnet stood with its users. The trader's needs that were not met and the usability issues in Liquidnet 5 showed our client where they could improve.
Different workflows
Liquidnet's users have vastly different workflows and trading styles. The product should be tailored to these user types instead of forcing all users to trade the same way.
Analytics
Data and information is at the heart of trading. Traders want proprietary information from Liquidnet to decide if they or their team should trade on Liquidnet or at all.
Usability
Liquidnet 5 has many hidden features that do not match current design heuristics or usability standards. This makes their product and new features less friendly to traders.

MOVING FORWARD
After meeting with our clients, our team narrowed down on our problem scope to focus on developing alert design guidelines and prototypes. We began to exploring how to turn our insights into the backbone of our design to ensure the user was central to our work at all times.
What we did
We gathered our product model, stakeholder map, affinity diagrams, and user interview notes together and posted them on a board to look through all at once. Then, we took some time to create new insights and re-emphasized the most important user needs.
What we learned
  • Quality of Professional Network: I want my leads to come from an exclusive source.
  • Data Analytics & Visualization: I need to be able to customize my work station.
  • Workflow: To do research, I need to be on multiple platforms which is taxing.
What we did
We generated questions that tried to answer the problems we found through walking the wall. The main goal here was to try to be specific enough to answer the user needs we identified, but also broad enough to create a whole design solution around it.
Example questions
  • How can we keep traders mentally alert to optimize their productivity?
  • How can traders be willing to revise their routines without sacrificing efficiency?
  • How can we design something visually articulate but familiar to traders?
  • How can we help traders better tackle work-life balance/avoid burnout?
What we did
We made assumptions based on our users’ needs and reversed each one. Then, we used this reversal to brainstorm a new service or feature offering. This set up the stage for preliminary solution exploration.
What we learned
  • Quality of Professional Network: I want my leads to come from an exclusive source.
  • Data Analytics & Visualization: I need to be able to customize my work station.
  • Workflow: To do research, I need to be on multiple platforms which is taxing.
What we did
We created 15 "How Might We" statements to sum up our most important problem areas and user needs. We plotted these onto a matrix and considered Liquidnet’s feasibility of building the potential product and also how much value this product could bring to our client. We ended up choosing 5 major questions we wanted to solve below.
How might we...
  • ... get traders to participate in making the product better?
  • ... keep traders mentally alert to optimize their productivity?
  • ... automate the right analytics fields to visualize data better?
  • ... reorient traders with where they left off at work after a hiatus?
  • ... get traders to avoid snowballing when they’re at a disadvantage?
Takeaways
Overall, this phase of research was able to turn our team from financial novices to traders-in-training. We deeply understood both the trader's needs and our client's goals in designing for the next generation of institutional traders. Our charge was now to utilize our research insights in our design phase to develop front-end style prototypes.
Progress

Our team narrowed our project focus based on our client's feedback and the research gathered. Future phases will focus on designing for Liquidnet's front-end and alerts system.

Learnings

Based on the data provided by our clients and interviews onsite, we discovered that front-end interactions and Liquidnet's new 'alert stream' may be the best avenue to design for.
